Analysis of Netflix Movie Genre Popularity in the United States

Motivation

The rise of streaming platforms like Netflix has led to an explosion in the number of movies and TV shows available to the public. With so much content to choose from, it can be overwhelming for viewers to make informed decisions about what to watch. Our visualization tool aims to address this problem by providing users with a way to better understand the landscape of film and TV ratings and genres throughout the years. By visualizing data about the Motion Picture Association rating system, as well as genre and release year informa- tion, our tool will help users make more informed decisions about what to watch. Our tool is designed for movie enthusiasts and the general public who are interested in understanding the history and evolution of film and TV culture. It will support two domain tasks: identifying which genres tend to generate certain ratings and analyzing movie and TV show ratings throughout the years. By supporting these tasks, our tool will enable users to better understand the cultural trends and shifts in the film and TV industry, and make more informed decisions about what movies and TV shows to watch. This visualization tool will cater to movie enthusiasts and the general public to better illustrate the progression of movie and TV show ratings throughout the years, as well as Motion Picture Association ratings and general film and TV show genres available on Netflix. The tool will be able to display to the user the movie name, release year, ratings, genre, and more. The user will also be able to interact with the plot to zoom in on certain periods of interest, as well as being able to select desired filters. By using this tool, the public will be able to get a better understanding of the Motion Picture Association rating system, as well as movie culture shifts throughout the years.

Background

Data

The selected data comes from Kaggle, and is a compiled list of Netflix movies and TV shows, with related vari- ables such as cast, director, release date, ratings, and more (https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/shivamb/netflix-shows). There are no inherent biases in the dataset, other than not knowing the credibility of the Kaggle user who compiled the data. Ethical consid- erations could be social responsibility, however, there is no personal user data and the societal impacts of this dataset are low ethically speaking. To clean the dataset, we used Python to sift through missing values and omit unnecessary data. Additionally, rows with null values were dropped. We then filtered the data such that the only country included with data was the United States. These steps brought the row count from around 8,000, to roughly 1800. For consistency purposes, The date column was then transformed into a proper datetime format. The word ‘minutes’ was also removed from this column in order to have a cleaner appearance. A new column for complexity was also added based on the original description column, using the automated rate-ability index, roughly based on the character length of the description column. Then, the description column was dropped, as it would not be relevant for our analyses purposes. Finally, originally the “listed in” column, referring to the genre, was just a singular column that contained one or more genres that the project was listed in. To better be able to manipulate the data, we found every type of genre listed in the rows, and created its own column based on those findings. The new rows now show a 1 or 0 (yes or no), if it was listed in that specific genre or not. After cleaning, there were no unexpected values or outliers
